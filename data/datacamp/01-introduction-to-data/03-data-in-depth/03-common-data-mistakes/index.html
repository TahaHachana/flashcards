The Four Common Data Mistakes

What are the four most common mistakes people make when working with data?

  1. Not clearly defining the problem — the question the data is meant to answer is too vague or poorly scoped.
  2. Insufficient or wrong data — not collecting enough data, or collecting data that does not actually represent the target population.
  3. Lack of appropriate analysis — using incorrect methods, aggregations, or jumping to conclusions without proper analysis.
  4. Poor communication of results — failing to present findings in a way that is clear, accurate, and relevant to the audience.

Mistake 1 — Not Clearly Defining The Problem

Why is clearly defining the problem or question the most important first step in any data project?

A vague question leads to vague data collection, flawed analysis, and ultimately incorrect conclusions. A well-defined question determines what data to collect, how to analyze it, and what a valid answer looks like. Without this clarity, every subsequent stage of the data life cycle is at risk of being misdirected.

Vague Vs Specific Questions

A researcher wants to understand the purchase habits of a group of customers. Evaluate the following questions and explain which is better and why:

A) "Did you buy anything in the last month?" B) "Which payment method did you use for your last purchase?"

B is better. Question A is too broad — it gives a general sense of whether purchases occurred but yields no actionable insight into how or where customers buy. Question B is specific and targeted, producing data that can directly answer a business question about payment behavior. A precise question prevents inappropriate data collection and keeps the analysis focused.

Consequences Of A Poorly Defined Question

What three risks arise from failing to clearly define the problem before collecting data?

  1. Inappropriate data collection — gathering data that does not actually address the intended question.
  2. Flawed analysis — analyzing the wrong variables or using the wrong methods because the goal was unclear.
  3. Incorrect conclusions — reaching answers that do not reflect reality because the question, data, and analysis were misaligned from the start.

Mistake 2 — Insufficient Or Wrong Data

What distinguishes "insufficient data" from "wrong data," and why does each undermine analysis?

  • Insufficient data — not enough records to draw statistically reliable conclusions; small samples produce unstable results.
  • Wrong data — data that does not represent the target population, even if the sample size is large. For example, surveying mostly young adults when the research question is about elderly people produces a biased dataset that cannot answer the intended question.

Both issues produce findings that appear valid but are actually unreliable.

Data Bias In Practice

A researcher studies the payment habits of elderly people using an online survey. Most responses come from young adults. What type of error is this, and what is its consequence?

This is an example of data bias — specifically a sampling bias. The responses are not representative of the target audience (elderly people), because the data collection method (an online survey) naturally skews toward younger, more digitally active respondents.

The consequence is that the data may yield insights about young adults' payment habits, but it cannot answer the original research question about elderly people. Any conclusions drawn would be misleading.

Mistake 3 — Lack Of Appropriate Analysis

Even with the right question and the right data, what can go wrong in the analysis stage?

Several errors can undermine the analysis stage: 1. Jumping to conclusions without applying proper statistical methods. 2. Lack of context — failing to account for external factors that explain the data. 3. Incorrect aggregations or calculations — using the wrong formulas or summarizations. 4. Confusing correlation with causation — assuming that a statistical relationship implies a direct causal link.

Lack Of Context In Analysis

Researchers observe a steep decline in contactless payments and conclude that elderly people are less inclined to use this payment method. What is wrong with this conclusion?

The researchers failed to account for data context. Unknown to them, widespread technical issues with payment terminals during that week were the actual cause of the decline — not a behavioral preference among elderly people. Without investigating the broader circumstances surrounding the data, they misattributed an external factor to a demographic trend.

This illustrates how lack of context leads to incorrect conclusions even from accurate data.

Confusing Correlation With Causation In Analysis

How can confusing correlation with causation lead to a wrong analytical conclusion? Give an example in the context of this lesson.

Correlation means two variables change together statistically; causation means one directly causes the other. Assuming correlation implies causation is a common analysis mistake.

In the payment habits example: a researcher might observe that elderly people and low contactless payment rates appear together in the data (correlation) and conclude that age causes lower contactless usage (causation). But the actual cause was a technical failure with payment terminals — a third variable entirely. Acting on the false causal conclusion could lead to misguided business decisions, such as removing contactless options for elderly customers.

Mistake 4 — Poor Communication Of Results

Why is poor communication of results considered especially costly, and what are its two main consequences?

Poor communication renders the entire preceding work — data collection, cleaning, and analysis — worthless in practice, since findings that are not understood cannot be acted upon. Its two main consequences are: 1. Wasted effort — insights that exist but are never used. 2. Misunderstandings or incorrect conclusions — stakeholders may misinterpret results and make poor decisions based on them.

Communication Mistake — Technical Complexity

A data analyst uses sophisticated statistical techniques to analyze payment habits but the manager cannot follow the analysis. What went wrong and how could it be avoided?

The analyst failed to tailor the communication to the audience. A technically rigorous analysis is only valuable if the recipient can understand its relevance to their business question. To avoid this: - Translate technical findings into plain business language. - Focus on what the result means for decisions, not how it was computed. - Use visuals and summaries rather than raw statistical output.

Understanding your audience is as important as the analysis itself.

Communication Mistake — Cherry-Picking And Misleading Charts

What is cherry-picking in data communication, and why is it a serious ethical problem?

Cherry-picking means selectively presenting only the data points that support a desired conclusion while ignoring contradicting evidence. It is a serious ethical problem because: 1. It misrepresents what the data actually shows. 2. It can lead stakeholders to make decisions based on a false picture. 3. It violates the principles of transparency and honest data use.

Using misleading chart types — such as truncated axes or distorted scales — has the same effect and is equally problematic.

Communication Mistake — Visualization Quality

What four visualization elements, when missing, increase the chance of misinterpretation?

  1. Clear labels — identifying what each data series or element represents.
  2. Legends — explaining colors, symbols, or line styles used in the chart.
  3. Axis titles — specifying what each axis measures and in what units.
  4. Appropriate colors — distinguishing between categories clearly and accessibly.

Without these elements, even an accurate visualization can be misread by the audience.

How Planning Ahead Prevents Data Mistakes

How does investing more time in the planning stage of the data life cycle reduce the likelihood of common data mistakes?

Thorough planning addresses the root cause of most data mistakes before they occur: - A clearly defined question prevents vague data collection and misaligned analysis. - Identifying the target population in advance reduces sampling bias. - Choosing analysis methods upfront ensures they suit the data type and research question. - Planning how to present findings to specific stakeholders improves communication from the start.

Most downstream problems in a data project can be traced back to insufficient planning.
