The Etymology Of Data

What is the Latin origin of the word "data" and what does it mean?

"Data" comes from the Latin word "datum," meaning "fact." In today's world, facts are incredibly valuable — but only when we know their meaning.

Definition Of Data

What is data?

Data is raw facts or observations in any form — numbers, text, images, or sounds — that have little meaning until they are given context or processed.

Data Is Everywhere

Give four examples that illustrate how data surrounds us in everyday life.

  1. Personal attributes — your name, age, and hobbies.
  2. Social media — comments, videos, and photos.
  3. Urban metrics — the number of trees in a city.
  4. Scientific measurements — the distance between Earth and the moon.

Data Context

What is data context, and why is it essential?

Data context is information about the circumstances and conditions surrounding data — such as units, timing, or source — that determines its meaning and appropriate use. Without context, data points cannot be correctly interpreted (e.g., comparing goals scored by a footballer and a hockey player tells us nothing about who is "better" without knowing the rules of each sport).

Metadata

What is metadata and how does it relate to data context?

Metadata is data that describes other data — such as when, where, how, or by whom it was collected. It is a key component of data context, providing the background needed to correctly interpret the underlying data.

Structured Data

What is structured data? Give three real-world examples.

Structured data is data organized in a predefined format — like tables, rows, and columns — that is easy to filter, query, and analyze. Examples: 1. Sales records in a spreadsheet. 2. Employee attendance logs. 3. Weather data recorded in a database table.

Unstructured Data

What is unstructured data, and what makes it challenging to work with?

Unstructured data is data without a fixed schema or organization — such as videos, images, or free-text — that requires processing before traditional analysis can be applied. Its lack of labels and order makes it harder to analyze directly, though it can offer rich insights once processed.

Structured Vs Unstructured Data

Which type of data is inherently better — structured or unstructured?

Neither is inherently better. The choice depends on the need: - Structured data (e.g., a goals table) is easier to filter and analyze directly. - Unstructured data (e.g., a match highlight video) is harder to analyze but can offer richer qualitative insights when properly processed.

Quantitative Data

What is quantitative data? Give three examples.

Quantitative data (also called numerical data) involves numbers that express amounts or measurements, making it suitable for calculations and statistical analysis. Examples: 1. Points scored in a game. 2. A person's height. 3. Daily temperature readings.

Qualitative Data

What is qualitative data? Give three examples.

Qualitative data (also called categorical data) is non-numerical, descriptive data that captures categories, attributes, opinions, or labels, used to identify patterns and meanings. Examples: 1. Favorite sport. 2. Customer feedback comments. 3. Product color categories.

Quantitative Vs Qualitative Data

What is the core difference between quantitative and qualitative data?

The difference lies in the type of information they provide: - Quantitative data is numerical — ideal for calculations and visualizations (e.g., temperature, scores). - Qualitative data is categorical — useful for spotting patterns and understanding meaning (e.g., favorite sports, customer opinions).

Note: this is distinct from the structured/unstructured distinction, which is about how data is presented, not what kind of information it carries.

The DIKW Pyramid

What is the DIKW pyramid and what are its four levels in order?

The DIKW pyramid is a mental model describing how raw data is refined through context and understanding to support better decisions. Its four levels from bottom to top are:

  1. Data — raw facts or observations.
  2. Information — data given context and meaning.
  3. Knowledge — information understood and internalized.
  4. Wisdom — knowledge applied to make sound decisions.

Data Quality

What is data quality and what four properties define it?

Data quality is the degree to which data is fit for its intended purpose. It is assessed across four properties: 1. Accurate — correctly reflects reality. 2. Complete — no important values are missing. 3. Valid — conforms to expected formats and rules. 4. Consistent — does not contradict other data in the dataset.
