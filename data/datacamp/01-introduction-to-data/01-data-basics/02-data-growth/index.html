Global Data Volume In 2010

How much data was created, captured, consumed, and stored globally in 2010?

Approximately 2 zettabytes of data were created, captured, consumed, and stored in 2010. (Source: Statista)

Exponential Growth Of Data Volume

By how much did global data volume grow between 2010 and 2021?

Global data volume grew approximately 40 times between 2010 and 2021 — from around 2 zettabytes in 2010 to roughly 80 zettabytes by 2021. This represents exponential, not linear, growth.

Why Data Storage Keeps Changing

Why does the way data is stored constantly change?

As the volume of data grows exponentially, existing storage methods become insufficient. New technologies are developed to store more data more efficiently — for example, digital videos didn't exist a few decades ago and required entirely new storage formats. New methods will continue to emerge as data volume keeps increasing.

Evolution Of Data Storage Timeline

List the major milestones in data storage history in chronological order.

  1. DNA — nature has always stored complete genetic information this way.
  2. Cave and wall paintings — earliest human record-keeping (thousands of years ago).
  3. Papyrus and parchment scrolls — used by Chinese, Egyptian, and Roman civilizations to track financial systems.
  4. Punch cards — popularized in the 1890s.
  5. Magnetic tape and floppy disks — replaced punch cards.
  6. CDs, hard drives, and solid state drives (SSDs) — high-capacity local digital storage.
  7. Cloud storage — data centralized in data centers via the Internet; one of today's most popular methods.

Cloud Storage

What is cloud storage and what problem does it solve?

Cloud storage is the centralization of data in remote data centers accessed via the Internet. It emerged alongside the growth of the Internet and helps balance server utilization across infrastructure. It addresses the challenge of storing ever-growing data volumes without requiring individuals or companies to maintain large local storage capacity.

Ice Cream Shop One — Gut Feeling Approach

How does ice cream shop one make decisions, and what is the consequence?

Shop one sells only three fixed flavors (vanilla, chocolate, strawberry). When a flavor runs out, it simply refills it the next day without questioning which flavors sell best or why. This gut-feeling approach leads to suboptimal decisions — such as keeping poor-selling flavors — and potentially losing sales.

Ice Cream Shop Two — Data-Driven Approach

What practices make ice cream shop two data-driven?

Shop two: - Sells 20+ flavors with a spare box of each kept in stock. - Uses an electronic cash register to monitor every sale. - Scans barcodes to continuously measure stock levels. - Collects weather data to anticipate demand changes. - Combines cost and revenue data to calculate margins per product.

This allows it to avoid stockouts, replace poor-selling flavors, predict sales spikes, and optimize pricing.

Types Of Data Captured And Their Uses

What four types of data does shop two capture, and what business decision does each support?

| Data Type | Business Decision | |---|---| | Sales data per product/flavor | Identify top and poor sellers | | Stock data (barcode scanning) | Avoid running out of popular flavors | | Weather data | Predict sales spikes on hot days | | Cost and revenue data | Calculate margins and adjust pricing |

Gut Feeling Vs Data-Driven Decisions

Why do data-driven decisions outperform gut-feeling decisions in the long run?

Gut feeling often leads to suboptimal choices — for example, replacing the wrong ice cream flavors and losing sales as a result. Data-driven decisions are grounded in evidence, making them more reliable and repeatable. In the long term, optimizing decisions through data results in increased revenue and operational efficiency.

Operational Complexity And Data Volume

What is the relationship between a company's operational complexity and the amount of data it generates?

As operational complexity increases, so does the volume of data generated and stored. A simple shop tracking three flavors generates little data; a shop tracking 20+ flavors, stock levels, weather, and margins generates far more. This scales further in large organizations.

Industry Examples Of Data Generation

Give two industry examples — beyond retail — that illustrate how complex operations generate large volumes of data.

  1. 3D manufacturing — sensors and tools continuously measure beam heat, layer thickness, and structural stability during production.
  2. Financial institutions — data is used to process mortgage applications and to detect fraudulent transactions in real time.

Data-Driven Decision Making Definition

What is data-driven decision making?

Data-driven decision making is a repeatable process of informing choices through evidence rather than intuition. Its key steps are: 1. Ask the right questions. 2. Collect and prepare the relevant data. 3. Perform analysis on the data. 4. Interpret results to guide the decision.

Aggregation

What is data aggregation? Give four examples of common aggregation metrics.

Aggregation is the process of summarizing many individual data points into simpler metrics to make large datasets interpretable. Common aggregation metrics: 1. Sum — e.g., total sales revenue. 2. Average — e.g., mean daily temperature. 3. Minimum / Maximum — e.g., lowest and highest selling flavors. 4. Mode — e.g., most frequently purchased flavor.

In the ice cream shop example, aggregating sales per flavor is what enables decisions about which products to keep or replace.
