Why Raw Data Is Hard To Work With

Why is raw data often too large to work with directly, and what must be done instead?

Even simple analytical tasks can require hundreds or thousands of records; complex tasks can involve millions or billions. Working with data at that scale in its raw form is impractical. To make informed decisions, data must be summarized into smaller, more interpretable pieces of information — a process achieved through aggregation.

What Is Aggregation

What is data aggregation and what goal does it achieve?

Aggregation is the process of summarizing many individual data points into simpler metrics to make large datasets interpretable. It translates raw data into a more usable form, allowing analysis to focus on specific attributes of a dataset rather than being overwhelmed by individual records.

Common Aggregation Types

Name five common aggregation types and give a brief description of each.

  1. Average (mean) — the central value of a dataset; the most commonly encountered aggregation in daily life.
  2. Sum (total) — the combined value of all data points.
  3. Minimum — the smallest value in a dataset.
  4. Maximum — the largest value in a dataset.
  5. Mode — the most frequently occurring value in a dataset.

Aggregations As Metrics And KPIs

How do aggregations relate to metrics, benchmarks, and KPIs within organizations?

Metrics, benchmarks, and KPIs are all aggregations at their core. They distill complex business processes into something measurable and actionable. For example, an average order value or a monthly churn rate is simply a structured aggregation applied to business data with a specific purpose — tracking performance against a goal.

Understanding Business Logic Behind Aggregations

Why is it important to understand the business logic behind a KPI or aggregation, and who tends to overlook this?

Every aggregation is built on assumptions — about which records are included, how values are calculated, and what the metric is designed to measure. Overlooking these assumptions can lead to misinterpreting results or drawing wrong conclusions.

Both new and experienced data practitioners commonly overlook this. Knowing how an aggregation is constructed — and what assumptions it makes — is surprisingly critical information for any investigation or analysis.

Curiosity As A Data Skill

How does curiosity help a data practitioner extract more value from aggregations and reports?

Being curious means not taking aggregations at face value. Useful questions include: - Where does this report's data originate? - What assumptions underlie this KPI? - Why was this benchmark set this way?

Tracing a metric back to its source and understanding its construction leads to a richer understanding of what the data actually represents — and reveals opportunities others might miss.

Data Flow In Organizations

Describe how data typically flows within an organization.

Data flow in organizations is often highly complex. Data originates from many different source systems, is then processed by other systems, and finally displayed in entirely different systems (such as dashboards or reports). Managing these complex pipelines — and creating a unified approach to using data as a resource — is the goal of the field of data management.

The Four Data Management Domains

What are the four most common domains within data management, and what does each oversee?

  1. Data Governance — ensures data is consistent, trustworthy, and not misused.
  2. Data Quality — ensures data is accurate, valid, complete, and consistent.
  3. Data Privacy — oversees how data is accessed and used in compliance with individuals' rights.
  4. Data Security — protects data from unauthorized access, breaches, or misuse.

Privacy and security work closely together, as both deal with controlling and protecting sensitive data.

Data Governance Domain

What is the purpose of the data governance domain within an organization?

Data governance ensures that an organization's data is consistent, trustworthy, and not misused. It establishes the policies, roles, and processes that define how data is managed, who is accountable for it, and what standards apply — forming the foundation that makes all other data domains work reliably.

Data Quality Domain

What four properties does the data quality domain aim to ensure, and why does quality matter for aggregations?

The data quality domain ensures data is: 1. Accurate — correctly reflects reality. 2. Valid — conforms to expected formats and rules. 3. Complete — no important values are missing. 4. Consistent — does not contradict itself across the dataset.

Quality is especially critical for aggregations: a sum or average calculated from inaccurate or incomplete records will produce a misleading metric, which then flows into flawed KPIs and poor decisions.

Shared Responsibility For Data

Who is responsible for treating data as a valuable resource within an organization?

Responsibility for data is shared across the entire organization — not just data teams or IT departments. Everyone who relies on data has a role in ensuring it is used appropriately and managed well. Developing an awareness of how data is used — and sometimes misused — is an essential skill for any data practitioner.

Key Performance Indicator Formal Definition

What is a KPI, and what makes understanding its construction critical?

A Key Performance Indicator (KPI) is a measurable metric derived from data that tracks performance against strategic goals or benchmarks. At its core, every KPI is an aggregation.

Understanding its construction is critical because a KPI is only meaningful if you know: - What data feeds into it. - What assumptions were made when designing it. - What business question it was built to answer.

Without this knowledge, a KPI can be misread or misapplied.

Data Governance Formal Definition

What is data governance?

Data governance is the set of policies, roles, and processes that ensure data is consistent, trustworthy, and used responsibly across an organization. It establishes accountability for data assets and defines the standards that all other data management domains operate within.

Least-Privilege Access

What is least-privilege access, and which data management domain does it support?

Least-privilege access is a security principle that grants users the minimum permissions required to perform their job — no more. It directly supports the data security and data privacy domains by limiting exposure of sensitive data: if a user cannot access data they don't need, they also cannot accidentally or intentionally misuse it.

GraphQL And Data Pipelines

What is GraphQL, and how does it relate to the complex data flows found in organizations?

GraphQL is a query language and runtime for APIs that allows clients to request exactly the data they need and aggregate responses in a single request — rather than fetching entire datasets and filtering afterward.

In the context of organizational data flows — where data moves from source systems through processing layers to display systems — GraphQL is one tool that helps manage efficient, precise data retrieval across those pipelines, reducing over-fetching and simplifying how systems communicate.
