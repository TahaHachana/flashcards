The Scale Of Daily Decision Making

How many decisions does an adult face each day, and what range of decisions does this include?

Adults face an estimated 35,000 decisions every day — ranging from trivial choices (what to eat, what to wear) to major, life-defining ones (what to study, where to live). Each carries its own set of consequences and expectations.

Definition Of Decision Making

What is decision making?

Decision making is the process we all undergo to make the right choices at the right time. For many decisions, employing data can help make a tough choice clearer — transforming uncertainty into an informed, evidence-based conclusion.

The Five Steps Of Data-Driven Decision Making

What are the five steps of the data-driven decision making process, in order?

  1. Ask — identify and clearly define the question you want to answer.
  2. Collect — gather the right data to answer that question.
  3. Prepare — clean and format the data for analysis.
  4. Analyze — apply tools and techniques to extract insights.
  5. Decide — interpret results and make an informed decision.

The Iterative Nature Of Data-Driven Decisions

Why is the data-driven decision making process described as iterative?

The process is iterative because the outcomes of decisions feed back into future decision making. As you observe results, you gain new information that allows you to refine your questions, improve your data collection, and make better decisions over time. Each cycle makes the next one more effective.

Step 1 — Asking The Right Question

Why is asking the right question considered the hardest step in the data-driven decision making process?

A clearly defined question determines everything that follows. A vague or poorly scoped question leads to collecting the wrong data, analyzing the wrong things, and ultimately making poor decisions. Taking extra time to precisely define the question prevents scope creep — drifting into unrelated areas — and sets the entire process up for success.

Step 2 — Collecting Data

What two considerations make the data collection step more effective?

  1. Deliberate sourcing — data often lives in multiple locations and forms, so being intentional about where you source it ensures you get the right data for your question.
  2. Thinking ahead to analysis — for example, choosing between raw data and data summarized by month before collection can reduce the amount of cleaning and preparation needed in the next step.

Step 3 — Preparing Data

What does data preparation involve, and how time-consuming can it be?

Data preparation involves two main activities: 1. Cleaning — converting messy or low-quality data into higher-quality data through skillful manipulation. 2. Formatting — arranging the data into the specific structure required by the chosen analysis method.

Data preparation can be the most time-consuming phase — in some cases taking up to 80% of the total time of the entire decision making process.

Step 4 — Analyzing Data

What is the purpose of the analysis step, and what are six common tools used for it?

Analysis transforms data into something actionable — insights that can inform a decision. Common data analysis tools include: 1. Python 2. R 3. Tableau 4. Power BI 5. Excel 6. Google Sheets

Each tool supports different types of analysis; the right choice depends on the complexity of the task and the analyst's skillset.

Step 5 — Making The Decision

What two inputs should be blended when making a data-driven decision, and why?

A data-driven decision should blend: 1. Analysis outcomes — the objective insights extracted from the data. 2. Personal experience and subject matter knowledge — contextual understanding that the data alone cannot capture.

Combining both produces a decision far more powerful than either gut feeling or data alone. Neither input should be ignored — the analysis grounds the decision in evidence, while experience provides judgment and nuance.

Why Gut Feeling Alone Is Insufficient

Why is relying solely on gut feeling for decisions problematic, according to the data-driven framework?

Gut feeling is subjective and unverifiable — it cannot be tested, repeated, or improved systematically. Data-driven decision making is a repeatable process: each cycle produces observations that refine future decisions. Gut feeling offers no such feedback loop, making it increasingly unreliable as decisions grow in complexity or scale.

Data Quality

What is data quality and why is it especially critical during the data preparation step?

Data quality is the degree to which data is accurate, complete, valid, and consistent for its intended purpose.

During preparation, low-quality data must be cleaned and transformed before analysis can begin. A dataset that is inaccurate, incomplete, or inconsistently formatted will produce unreliable insights — and poor insights lead to poor decisions, regardless of how sophisticated the analysis tools are. This is why preparation can consume up to 80% of total project time.

Data-Driven Decision Making — Formal Definition

What is the formal definition of data-driven decision making?

Data-driven decision making is a repeatable process of asking questions, collecting and preparing the right data, performing analysis, and interpreting results to inform choices — rather than relying on intuition alone.

The key word is repeatable: each decision cycle generates new data that improves the next, creating a continuous improvement loop.

Correlation Vs Causation

What is the difference between correlation and causation, and why does it matter during the analysis and decision steps?

  • Correlation — a statistical relationship where two variables change together, but neither necessarily causes the other.
  • Causation — one variable directly causes changes in another.

The two must never be assumed equivalent. During analysis, mistaking correlation for causation can produce a confident-looking but fundamentally wrong insight — leading to decisions that act on a false cause. For example, concluding that a marketing campaign caused a sales spike when both were actually driven by a third factor (e.g., seasonality).
